🌱 Gregor Mendel & the Foundations of Genetics

In the 1850s, an Austrian monk named Gregor Mendel began crossing pea plants in his monastery garden. He tracked traits like seed shape, flower color, and plant height across thousands of plants and multiple generations. From his observations, he developed three laws that still form the foundation of genetics today — we now call them Mendel's Laws of Inheritance.

Why pea plants?Pea plants were the perfect research organism: they naturally self-pollinate (giving Mendel control over crosses), they produce large numbers of offspring quickly, and their traits come in clear "either-or" forms — seeds are either round or wrinkled, flowers are either purple or white. Mendel also started with purebred plants that always produced the same trait generation after generation.

⚖️ The Three Laws

The Law of Dominance explains that when two different alleles are present, one — the dominant allele — will be expressed, while the other — the recessive allele — is hidden. We write dominant alleles as uppercase letters (e.g., B) and recessive alleles as lowercase (e.g., b). A person with genotype Bb will show the dominant trait, because B masks b.

The Law of Segregation describes what happens when gametes (eggs and sperm) are formed during meiosis. The two alleles an organism carries for each gene separate, so each gamete carries only ONE allele. When fertilization occurs, the new organism inherits one allele from each parent — restoring the pair.

The Law of Independent Assortment tells us that genes for different traits sort into gametes independently of each other — provided those genes are on different chromosomes. The alleles you inherit for hair color have nothing to do with the alleles you inherit for height.

🔑 Essential Vocabulary

A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a protein, and ultimately, a trait. An allele is one version of a gene. Every individual carries two alleles for each gene — one from each parent. When both alleles are the same, the individual is homozygous (BB or bb). When the alleles differ, the individual is heterozygous (Bb).

The combination of alleles an organism carries is its genotype. The physical, observable trait that results is the phenotype. Here is the crucial insight: two organisms with different genotypes (BB and Bb) can have the identical phenotype — both show the dominant trait, even though one carries a hidden recessive allele.

Key insight — Hidden Alleles:Two brown-haired parents (both Bb) can have a blonde child (bb). Each parent carries a hidden recessive allele that doesn't show in their phenotype. When meiosis separates their alleles and fertilization combines them, there is a 1-in-4 chance both parents pass their b allele to the same child.

How to Do a Monohybrid Cross
5-step process — then try the interactive simulator
1

Identify the trait and assign letters

Choose one letter to represent the gene. The UPPERCASE letter = dominant allele; the lowercase letter = recessive allele.

ExampleIn pea plants, round seeds (R) are dominant to wrinkled seeds (r). R = round (dominant), r = wrinkled (recessive).
2

Write the parent genotypes

Based on the problem description, write out each parent's two alleles. "Homozygous dominant" = two uppercase (RR). "Homozygous recessive" = two lowercase (rr). "Heterozygous" = one of each (Rr).

Example"Cross a heterozygous plant with a wrinkled-seed plant" → Parent 1: Rr, Parent 2: rr
3

Find the gametes each parent can make

Split each parent's genotype into its two alleles — these are the possible gametes. The Law of Segregation says each gamete gets only ONE allele.

ExampleRr → gametes: R and r  |  rr → gametes: r and r (only one type)
4

Set up and fill in the Punnett square

Write one parent's gametes across the TOP of a 2×2 grid. Write the other parent's gametes down the SIDE. Fill each box by combining the letter from above with the letter from the side (put the uppercase letter first).

ExampleRr × rr → Top: R, r. Side: r, r. Fill boxes: Rr | rr | Rr | rr
5

Calculate genotypic AND phenotypic ratios

Genotypic ratio: Count each unique genotype. Write as a ratio or percentage (e.g., 1 RR : 2 Rr : 1 rr).

Phenotypic ratio: Group genotypes by what they LOOK like. Any genotype with at least one uppercase (dominant) allele shows the dominant phenotype. Only a homozygous recessive (rr) shows the recessive phenotype.

Example for Rr × rrBoxes: Rr, rr, Rr, rr → Genotypic: 2 Rr : 2 rr (1:1) → Phenotypic: 2 Round : 2 Wrinkled (1:1) = 50% Round, 50% Wrinkled

How to Do a Dihybrid Cross
Two traits at once — FOIL your way through it
1

Assign letters for both traits

Each trait gets its own letter. Keep them clearly separate — never mix up the letters from different traits when filling the grid.

ExampleTrait 1: Tall (T) vs. short (t)  |  Trait 2: Round (R) vs. wrinkled (r). A dihybrid parent is written: TtRr
2

Use FOIL to list all 4 gametes

A dihybrid parent (TtRr) can make 4 different gametes. Use FOIL from algebra to list them systematically: First · Outer · Inner · Last.

FOIL for TtRrFirst: T·R = TR  |  Outer: T·r = Tr  |  Inner: t·R = tR  |  Last: t·r = tr → Gametes: TR, Tr, tR, tr
3

Build a 4×4 Punnett square

Write one parent's 4 gametes across the top. Write the other parent's 4 gametes down the side. You will have 16 boxes to fill. Combine each top gamete + side gamete to fill each box (dominant alleles first).

4

Count phenotypes — ask two questions per box

For each of the 16 boxes: (1) Does it have at least one T? (2) Does it have at least one R? This gives you four phenotype combinations.

The 9:3:3:1 RuleWhen BOTH parents are TtRr × TtRr: 9 Tall+Round : 3 Tall+Wrinkled : 3 Short+Round : 1 Short+Wrinkled. This specific 9:3:3:1 only appears with two dihybrid heterozygotes!

✅ Lesson 1 — Key Takeaways

Law of DominanceDominant alleles (uppercase) mask recessive alleles (lowercase). One copy of dominant = dominant phenotype. Two copies of recessive = recessive phenotype.
Law of SegregationAllele pairs separate during meiosis. Each gamete gets exactly ONE allele per gene. This is why Bb produces both B and b gametes.
Law of Independent AssortmentGenes on different chromosomes sort independently into gametes. Hair color alleles don't affect eye color alleles.
Monohybrid Cross Steps(1) Assign letters (2) Write parent genotypes (3) Find gametes (4) Fill 2×2 grid (5) Calculate genotypic and phenotypic ratios.
Dihybrid Cross — FOILFor TtRr: gametes are TR, Tr, tR, tr. Build a 4×4 grid. TtRr × TtRr always gives 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio.
Genotype ≠ PhenotypeBB and Bb look identical (both show dominant). Only bb shows recessive. You can have the same phenotype from different genotypes!
