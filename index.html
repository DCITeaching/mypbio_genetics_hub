Gregor Mendel & The Rules of Inheritance
Foundation — Lesson 1

In the 1850s, an Austrian monk named Gregor Mendel crossed pea plants in his monastery garden, tracking traits across thousands of plants. He developed three laws still foundational to genetics today. Pea plants were ideal: they self-pollinate, produce many offspring quickly, and have clear either-or traits (round vs. wrinkled, purple vs. white).

The Three LawsLaw of Dominance: dominant allele masks recessive when present. Law of Segregation: allele pairs separate during meiosis — each gamete gets ONE allele. Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes sort independently into gametes.

An allele is one version of a gene. Each individual carries two alleles per gene — one from each parent. If both alleles are the same, the individual is homozygous (RR or rr). If different, they are heterozygous (Rr). The allele combination an organism carries is its genotype; what you observe is its phenotype.

Key insight: BB and Bb have the same phenotype (both show dominant trait) but different genotypes. Two heterozygous parents (Bb × Bb) have a 25% chance of producing a recessive child (bb) — even though neither parent shows the recessive trait.

1b
Punnett Squares — Predicting Offspring
Monohybrid (2×2) and Dihybrid (4×4)

A Punnett square is a grid tool for predicting offspring genotype and phenotype probabilities. For a monohybrid cross (one trait), use a 2×2 grid. For a dihybrid cross (two traits), use a 4×4 grid.

Monohybrid steps: (1) Assign letters — uppercase for dominant, lowercase for recessive. (2) Write parent genotypes. (3) Split into gametes (each gamete gets ONE allele). (4) Fill the grid. (5) Calculate genotypic ratio (actual allele combos) and phenotypic ratio (what you see). Classic Rr × Rr gives 3 dominant : 1 recessive phenotype ratio.

Dihybrid FOIL method: For parent TtRr, use FOIL to get all 4 gametes: TR, Tr, tR, tr. Build a 4×4 grid (16 boxes). The classic TtRr × TtRr result is 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio.

2
Beyond Simple Dominance
Incomplete Dominance, Codominance, Multiple Alleles — Lesson 2

Most real traits don't follow simple dominant/recessive rules. Incomplete dominance: neither allele fully dominates — the heterozygote shows a blended phenotype. Example: red (RR) × white (R'R') snapdragons → ALL pink (RR') offspring. Crossing RR' × RR' gives 1 Red : 2 Pink : 1 White.

Codominance: both alleles are fully and simultaneously expressed — both phenotypes appear at once, no blending. Example: brown horse × white horse → appaloosa (brown spots ON white coat). Blood type AB is the key human example.

Critical DistinctionIncomplete dominance → NEW blended phenotype (pink). Codominance → BOTH original phenotypes visible at the same time (brown AND white). Never confuse these two!

Multiple alleles: more than 2 allele versions exist in the population for one gene (each individual still only has 2). ABO blood type has three alleles: A, B, and i. A and B are codominant with each other; both are dominant over i. Blood type O (ii) is actually the MOST COMMON blood type worldwide — being recessive does NOT mean being rare.

2b
Sex-Linked Traits & Polygenic Inheritance
X-linked genes and continuous variation

Humans have 22 pairs of autosomes and 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX = female; XY = male). X-linked traits are encoded on the X chromosome. Since males only have ONE X chromosome, any recessive allele on their X is automatically expressed — there is no second X to carry a masking dominant allele. This is why X-linked recessive traits (color blindness, hemophilia) affect males far more often than females.

Notation: write alleles as superscripts on X. Carrier female = XBXb (unaffected but can pass to sons). Affected male = XbY. Affected fathers CANNOT pass X-linked traits to sons — sons get the Y chromosome from dad.

Polygenic inheritance: a trait controlled by two or more genes, producing a continuous spectrum of phenotypes (bell curve). Examples: skin color, height, eye color. Linked genes: genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together, violating independent assortment (can only be separated by crossing over).

3
Mutations — When DNA Changes
Gene mutations, chromosome mutations, somatic vs. germ line — Lesson 3

A mutation is any permanent change in the DNA sequence. Mutations can be caused by replication errors, mutagens (UV light, radiation, cigarette smoke), or viruses. Not all mutations are harmful — many are neutral, and beneficial mutations are the engine of evolution.

Where Does the Mutation Happen?Somatic (body) cell mutations → affect only that individual; can cause cancer; CANNOT be inherited by offspring. Germ line (gamete-producing cell) mutations → can be inherited; every cell of the next generation carries it. This is how heritable disorders arise.

Gene mutations: Point mutation — ONE nucleotide substituted (may be silent, change one amino acid, or create early stop). Frameshift — insertion or deletion of nucleotides shifts the entire reading frame downstream. Frameshifts are usually MORE damaging than point mutations because every codon after the mutation is altered.

Chromosome mutations: Nondisjunction — chromosomes fail to separate in meiosis → wrong chromosome number in gametes → offspring with trisomy (e.g., Trisomy 21 = Down syndrome). Translocation — a segment moves to a different chromosome.

3b
Pedigrees — Reading Family Trees
Symbols, patterns, and what they mean

A pedigree traces a trait's inheritance across family generations. Squares = males; circles = females. Filled = affected; unfilled = unaffected; half-filled = carrier (sex-linked). Horizontal lines connect mates; vertical lines drop to offspring.

Autosomal Recessive: Trait skips generations; both sexes equally affected; two unaffected parents can have an affected child (both are carriers). Examples: cystic fibrosis, sickle cell.

Autosomal Dominant: Appears every generation; both sexes equally affected; unaffected individuals NEVER have affected children. Examples: Huntington's, achondroplasia.

X-linked Recessive: Far more males affected; trait skips generations through carrier females; affected father's sons are UNAFFECTED (sons get Y from dad, not his X). Examples: hemophilia, color blindness.

Key Diagnostic ClueIf an affected father's sons are all unaffected → X-linked (sons can't inherit dad's X-linked allele). This is the strongest single clue for X-linked inheritance.
4
The Molecular Toolkit
Genetic Engineering — Lesson 4

Genetic engineering: deliberately modifying an organism's DNA using biotechnology tools. Restriction enzymes are molecular scissors — they cut DNA at specific short sequences (recognition sites), creating sticky ends. DNA ligase is the glue — it joins fragments at their sticky ends. Together, restriction enzymes + ligase create recombinant DNA (DNA from two or more sources combined).

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): amplifies tiny DNA samples into millions of copies. Used in forensics, COVID testing, paternity testing. Gel electrophoresis: separates DNA by SIZE using electric current. Small fragments travel FARTHER. Produces banding patterns used for DNA fingerprinting. Always run a ladder (known sizes) alongside samples.

CRISPR-Cas9: the most precise gene-editing tool today. A guide RNA directs Cas9 to cut at a specific sequence; the cell's repair machinery can then delete, correct, or insert sequences. Works in virtually any organism.

4b
Applications & Bioethics
Real-world impact and Criterion D thinking

Bacterial transformation: human gene inserted into bacteria → bacteria produce human protein. Classic example: insulin (human insulin gene in bacteria since 1982). GMO crops: Bt corn (bacterial toxin gene reduces pesticide use), Golden Rice (beta-carotene for Vitamin A). Gene therapy: correct/replace mutated gene in a patient's own body cells (somatic — NOT inherited). Used for sickle cell, hemophilia, inherited blindness.

DNA fingerprinting: PCR + gel electrophoresis → unique banding profile per individual. Used in forensics, paternity testing, evolutionary comparison. Accuracy: 1 in billions.

Somatic vs. Germline EditingSomatic = affects one patient only; NOT inherited. Germline = affects embryo; ALL future descendants carry the change. Germline editing is ethically controversial and mostly banned. The He Jiankui case (2018 CRISPR babies) was globally condemned — the scientist went to prison.

Criterion D requires you to EVALUATE — not just describe. Distinguish therapeutic editing (cure disease) from enhancement editing (select traits). Consider consent, heritability, access/inequality, and who should decide.

📚 Study Strategies That Work

  • Spaced repetition: do flashcards for 10 min daily — not 70 min the night before
  • Practice testing: close notes and try a practice question BEFORE checking the answer
  • Teach-back: explain a concept out loud to a friend, family member, or your own reflection
  • Error analysis: when you get a practice question wrong, write WHY in the margin
  • Punnett practice: redo Rr × Rr and TtRr × TtRr from scratch without looking — 5 min/day
  • Concept mapping: draw how Lessons 1–4 connect — mutations → pedigrees → inheritance patterns

✏️ MYP Command Terms

  • Identify — name or recognize something (no explanation required)
  • Explain — give reasons with detail and mechanism ("because...")
  • Predict — use Punnett logic to state an expected outcome with probability
  • Determine — find the answer by calculation or reasoning from evidence
  • Evaluate — make a judgment AND defend it with evidence, multiple perspectives
  • Analyze — break down a pedigree, data set, or scenario into components and discuss relationships
  • Justify — give reasons/evidence that defend a conclusion
